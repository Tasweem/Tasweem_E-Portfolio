✥ Unit 1 - Scientific Investigation and Ethics

Outcomes from the Team Exercises and activites

.

n this unit, we delved into the scientific method's key problem-solving techniques, focusing on Deductive and Inductive reasoning—essential forms of inference in research. Deductive reasoning guides the researcher from a general idea to specific observations, while inductive reasoning reverses this path, leading from specific observations to broader generalizations. Deduction, as outlined by Miessler in 2020, relies on the accuracy of assumptions to reach a flawless conclusion, which can be challenging outside controlled scientific environments due to the potential lack of universally accepted facts. Induction, conversely, is more adaptable to daily problem-solving, especially when dealing with incomplete information, yielding practical albeit not infallible conclusions. Comprehending these reasoning types is critical before advancing in research, just as grasping research ethics is indispensable, particularly when human participants are involved.

Dawson (2015), discusses the complexity and personal nature of the intellectual discovery process in research, highlighting how inductive and deductive reasoning play crucial roles.

  • Inductive Reasoning: This involves starting from observations and drawing general conclusions to build models and theories, which depends on the data available, the subject being studied, and pre-existing knowledge and beliefs. It emphasizes how knowledge acquisition (epistemology) can either be generalized (positivism) or remain unique to the individual and situation (anti-positivism).
  • Deductive Reasoning: This starts with existing knowledge and understanding to predict observations that haven't been encountered before. It's shaped by one's theory of reality (ontology), personal understanding, and assumptions, leading to diverse deductions due to varying perspectives.

It also underscores the importance of ethical considerations in computer science and information systems projects, emphasizing that ethical issues are not limited to the medical or social sciences but also critically relevant in these fields.

Ethical considerations are highlighted across three primary dimensions: conducting research with integrity, managing participant involvement responsibly, and presenting results truthfully. Key points include the necessity of conducting research with honesty, respecting copyright, and intellectual property, being open to critique, and maintaining accuracy and honesty with all stakeholders involved.

Particularly when projects involve participants, especially vulnerable groups, ethical concerns intensify. The need for obtaining ethical clearance, respecting data protection laws (such as the UK's Data Protection Act of 1998), ensuring informed consent, and addressing confidentiality and security of data are paramount.

Reflection & Practical Use:

Reflecting on the interconnectedness of reasoning in research, ethical considerations, and the evolution of legal systems, one cannot help but appreciate the profound depth and intricacy inherent in the pursuit of knowledge and the administration of justice. The intellectual journey of research—whether it be in computer science or another field—requires a balance of inductive and deductive reasoning, a process deeply rooted in personal epistemology and ontology. It’s a delicate dance between what is observed and what is inferred, with each step guided by prior knowledge and the ethical imperative to conduct and present research with integrity.

-

As a Mauritian, it is true that Mauritius exemplifies the rich tapestry of legal pluralism, where English common law and French civil law converge, mirroring the island's historical narrative. This legal hybridity symbolizes a broader global theme: the coexistence of tradition and modernity. Just as research reflects personal and societal values, so too does law embody the collective conscience and evolving norms.

Overall, these themes underscore the importance of context—historical, cultural, ethical—in shaping how we understand and govern our world. Whether it’s formulating a hypothesis or crafting a law, the underlying processes are not merely technical but are imbued with human values, judgments, and the perpetual quest for a fair and equitable society.
