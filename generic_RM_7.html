Discussion:

The discourse on reliability, validity, and generalizability, coupled with an examination of qualitative and quantitative research methodologies, underscores their pivotal role in enhancing the integrity of research findings. Reliability is concerned with the consistency of research results across different contexts and among various researchers. It emphasizes the repeatability of outcomes and the precision of research techniques, aiming for stable and consistent results over time as highlighted by Trochim (2006). Validity delves into the accuracy of the research in achieving its intended objectives, incorporating both the robustness of the research design and the appropriate interpretation of outcomes. Creswell (2013) extends this notion, asserting that validity encompasses the entire research process, from the design to the execution, to ensure it genuinely reflects the studied phenomenon.

Generalizability, or external validity, refers to the extent to which research findings can be applied across different settings and populations. This concept is crucial for the broader applicability of research outcomes, as discussed by Babbie (2010), who stresses the importance of extending findings to a wider audience beyond the initial study sample. Qualitative research, which seeks to unravel the "what" and "why" behind human behavior, employs both descriptive and inferential statistics to analyze data. Starting with descriptive statistics helps in identifying patterns, setting the groundwork for methodologies that are not only accurate but also reflective of broader demographics. Together, these principles ensure that research methodologies are designed in a manner that is both rigorous and representative, thereby bolstering the credibility and utility of research findings in various contexts.
