✥Unit 4: Case Studies, Focus Groups and Observations

Outcomes from the Team Exercises and activites

The module introduces various data collection methods used predominantly in qualitative research, with potential applications in quantitative research. It covers the in-depth investigation through case studies, highlighting their utility in hypothesis generation and the exploration of phenomena, while cautioning against their use for establishing cause-effect relationships due to potential biases and the risk of atypical responses undermining generalizability. It also explores the use of focus groups for gathering rich, qualitative insights from a selected group of individuals sharing common characteristics, aimed at understanding the "why," "what," and "how" aspects of a topic. Furthermore, the distinction between quantitative and qualitative observations is made, with the former involving the measurement of numerical values and the latter focusing on monitoring characteristics in a natural setting to gain deeper insights. The module emphasizes the importance of selecting appropriate methods based on the research goals and the potential need to employ multiple methods for comprehensive data collection.

Discussion:

Dawson (2015) discusses four principal research methods: action research, experiments, case studies, and surveys, each serving distinct purposes in research.

Action Research emphasizes solving a specific problem within an organization and evaluating the outcomes, cautioning against focusing solely on action completion without proper evaluation (Herbert, 1990). It involves active participation and requires meticulous documentation and analysis.

Experiments are designed to explore causal relationships by manipulating variables in a controlled setting. Saunders et al. (2012) describe the experimental process, including hypothesis formulation, sample selection, and variable control. Challenges such as ethical concerns and sample access often necessitate quasi-experimental designs.

Case Studies provide an in-depth examination of situations or entities, employing both direct (e.g., interviews, observation) and indirect methods (e.g., document analysis) for data collection. Authors like Cornford and Smithson (2006), Berndtsson et al. (2008), Gerring (2007), and Yin (2014) stress the importance of analysing subjective data critically to derive meaningful and accurate conclusions, while being mindful of the researcher's influence.

Surveys involve collecting standardized data from a large population efficiently, typically through questionnaires or interviews. This method, detailed by Saunders et al. (2012), Czaja and Blair (1996) is valuable for its economic approach to data gathering from sizable groups.

The author also very clearly differentiates between cross-sectional and longitudinal studies, based on their time frames, highlighting the importance of choosing the right study type based on research objectives.

Furthermore, it underlines the significance of recognizing the researcher's potential impact on study participants, particularly in observational research, to avoid skewed data—a phenomenon exemplified by the Hawthorne effect.

In essence, these research methods offer varied avenues for inquiry, each with specific advantages and constraints. Selecting the appropriate method is crucial, depending on the research question, objectives, and context, to ensure the integrity and relevance of the findings.

Seminar:Title: Case Study on Privacy:

Please read the case below and answer the questions. You need to determine the ethical issues involved with this case.

The Case

Ricardo works for the records department of his local government as a computer records clerk, where he has access to files of property tax records. For a scientific study, a researcher, Beth, has been granted access to the numerical portion “but not the corresponding names” of some records.

Beth finds some information that she would like to use, but she needs the names and addresses corresponding with certain properties. Beth asks Ricardo to retrieve these names and addresses, so she can contact these people for more information and for permission to do further study.

Now consider, what are the ethical issues involved in deciding which of these options to pursue?

  • If Ricardo is not responsible for determining allowable access, should he release the names and addresses?
  • Suppose Ricardo were responsible for determining allowable access to the files. What ethical issues would be involved in his deciding whether to grant access to Beth?
  • Should Beth be allowed to contact the individuals involved? That is, should the Records department release individuals' names to a researcher? What are the ethical issues for the Records department to consider?
  • Suppose Beth contacts the individuals to ask their permission, and one-third of them respond giving permission, one-third respond denying permission, and one-third do not respond. Beth claims that at least one-half of the individuals are needed to make a valid study. What options are available to Beth?

Answer:

Privacy and Confidentiality

Ricardo, with access to property tax records, holds a position of trust, expected to safeguard the privacy and confidentiality of the individuals listed within these records. Unauthorized release of names and addresses breaches privacy rights, a principle that is staunchly protected under laws like the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in the European Union and the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) in the United States. Notably, there have been instances where individuals in similar positions faced legal consequences for unauthorized disclosure, emphasizing the severity of such breaches.

Consent and Authorization

Ricardo should refrain from releasing the names and addresses without proper authorization, as doing so would bypass established protocols designed to protect individual privacy. Should Ricardo have the authority to grant access, ethical and legal considerations must guide his decision, ensuring alignment with the individuals' rights to privacy, possibly requiring a review process or seeking consent from those involved.

Ethical Considerations for the Records Department

The Records department faces the ethical challenge of balancing the potential benefits of research against individuals' rights to privacy. There must be clear criteria for when information can be released, prioritizing studies that serve significant public interest and ensuring adequate safeguards for participants' confidentiality.

Contacting Individuals and Consent

Beth's intention to contact individuals introduces complexities regarding informed consent. It's paramount that individuals fully understand how their information will be used before agreeing to participate in the study. If only a fraction consents, Beth must reconsider her approach, potentially seeking additional participants or revising the study's scope to respect those consent boundaries.

Real-world Consequences and Legal Frameworks

The importance of adhering to privacy laws and ethical standards is underscored by real-world consequences, including imprisonment, fines, and reputational damage for breaches of personal information. Legal frameworks like GDPR and HIPAA, alongside ethical guidelines from professional bodies such as the American Psychological Association (APA) and the Association for Computing Machinery (ACM), provide the foundation for handling sensitive data responsibly. High-profile cases like the Cambridge Analytica scandal further highlight the risks and ethical implications of misusing personal data.

In summary, the ethical dilemmas faced by Ricardo and Beth resonate with broader concerns over privacy, consent, and data protection in the digital age. The principles of safeguarding privacy, obtaining informed consent, and adhering to authorized data access procedures are crucial for maintaining trust in institutions handling personal information. These principles, supported by legal and ethical frameworks, aim to protect individuals' rights while navigating the complexities of data management and research.



Reflection & Practical Use:

Integrating the discussions from the case study involving Ricardo and Beth with my reflections as a data analyst in the hospitality sector, the emphasis on privacy, consent, and ethical data management becomes even more pertinent. The discussions delve into the complexities of accessing and utilizing personal data for research or business purposes, highlighting the paramount importance of safeguarding individual privacy and ensuring informed consent. These principles are not just theoretical concerns but are deeply embedded in the day-to-day operations of the hospitality industry, where personal data is central to offering customized guest experiences.

The distinction between quantitative and qualitative research methods, as discussed, sheds light on the diverse approaches to data collection and analysis. In the hospitality sector, both methods are crucial. Quantitative data helps us understand patterns and trends in guest behaviour, while qualitative insights, such as those derived from focus groups or case studies, enrich our understanding of guest preferences and expectations. This dual approach underscores the need for a nuanced understanding of data collection methodologies and their ethical implications.

Moreover, the ethical considerations for the Records department, highlighted in the case, mirror the responsibilities of data managers in hospitality. We are tasked with balancing the benefits of data analysis with the imperative to protect guest privacy. This involves establishing clear criteria for data access and usage, ensuring that any data collection serves a legitimate purpose and that adequate safeguards are in place to protect guest information.

Reflecting on the discussions and the case of Ricardo and Beth from my perspective as a data analyst in the hospitality sector, it is clear that ethical data management is a cornerstone of building and maintaining guest trust. The discussions serve as a reminder of the importance of transparency, consent, and privacy protection in all data-related activities. They also highlight the necessity for ongoing education and vigilance to navigate the evolving legal and ethical landscape of data management. As data analysts, our role extends beyond mere analysis; we are also guardians of our guests' privacy, tasked with ensuring that the data insights we pursue are not only valuable for our business but also respectful of the individuals behind the data.
